A question that kept coming up...

"Why do some teams with average talent consistently outperform teams full of brilliant engineers?"

The answer isn't what I expected.
It wasn't about smarts. It was about how they acted on what they knew.

This led me to an important distinction...

Intellect vs Skill

Intellect

  • Raw cognitive horsepower
  • Pattern recognition & abstraction
  • Knowing what could be done
  • Potential energy
VS

Skill (Applied Agency)

  • Consistent execution under pressure
  • Turning insight into action
  • Knowing how to get it done
  • Kinetic energy

The gap between intellect and skill is bridged by agency — the willingness to act, iterate, and own outcomes.

Put another way...

The Core Insight

"Intelligence without action is just potential energy."

Smart but Passive

Sees problems, analyzes perfectly, writes great documents... but waits for permission to act.

Skilled and Active

Sees problems, proposes solutions, runs experiments, ships fixes, and retrospects.

The world doesn't reward what you know — it rewards what you do with what you know.

What happens

Here's what this looks like in our daily work...

Analysis Paralysis in Action

Scenario: Production deployments take 45 minutes and fail 30% of the time.

The Trap (Team A)

"Let's form a working group to evaluate CI/CD platforms. We need a comparison matrix of Jenkins, GitLab CI, GitHub Actions, ArgoCD, and Tekton across 40 criteria."

Week 4: Matrix 80% complete, debating weighting.
Week 8: Stakeholder review pushed to next quarter.
Week 12: Still 45 min. Still failing 30%.

VS

High Agency (Team B)

"I profiled our pipeline last night. 60% of time is pulling uncached Docker layers. I pushed a caching fix. Deploys are down to 18 minutes. Here's the data."

Day 1: Root cause found through action.
Day 2: Fix shipped, measurable improvement.
Day 5: Failure rate dropped to 8%.

Team A sought the perfect answer. Team B sought the fastest learning.

So if agency is the differentiator, how do we measure it?

The 5 Levels of Agency

in Platform Engineering

Every interaction with a problem can be mapped
to one of five distinct levels of agency.

Let's walk through each one with the same scenario:
a broken CI/CD pipeline.

Level 1 — Problem Identification

"The CI/CD pipeline is broken."

Characteristics

  • Simply reports that something is wrong
  • No investigation into cause or impact
  • Delegates the entire problem upward
  • Creates more work for leadership, not less

Agency Level: 20% — The bare minimum. But at least they spoke up.

A step up: they investigate before raising the alarm...

Level 2 — Cause Analysis

"The pipeline is broken because of an API credentials issue."

Characteristics

  • Investigates and identifies root cause
  • Shows technical competence
  • Still waits for someone else to fix it
  • Better, but still creates a dependency

Agency Level: 40% — Good diagnosis, but the ball is still in someone else's court.

Now they start proposing paths forward...

Level 3 — Solution Exploration

"We could roll back or update the credentials."

Characteristics

  • Proposes possible solutions
  • Shows initiative beyond diagnosis
  • Hasn't validated any options yet
  • Still needs a decision-maker to choose

Agency Level: 60% — Options without data. We can go further.

This is where the shift gets dramatic...

Level 4 — Solution Recommendation

"The API updated; I tested the fix in staging. Can I deploy?"

Characteristics

  • Investigated, validated, and tested a solution
  • Presents data-backed recommendation
  • Only needs a "yes" to proceed
  • Reduces leadership load dramatically

Agency Level: 80% — The leader's job becomes a 5-second approval.

And at the highest level, leadership isn't even needed...

Level 5 — Problem Resolution

"I updated the credentials, deployed the fix, and added monitoring."

Characteristics

  • Fully autonomous problem resolution
  • Proactively prevents recurrence
  • Communicates after the fact
  • Creates systemic improvement

Agency Level: 100% — The problem was solved before leadership even heard about it.

Let's see the full picture side by side...

Same problem. Five vastly different responses.

Engineers flagged foam strike risks but were overruled by hierarchy. Cost: 7 lives.

Moving up requires leaders to change how they respond to failure.

The key: distinguish between two fundamentally different kinds of failure...

Re-framing Failure

Preventable Failures

  • Known process violations
  • Ignoring best practices
  • Cutting corners on testing

Address through process improvement

VS

Intelligent Failures

  • Thoughtful experiments in new territory
  • Testing hypotheses with real data
  • Pushing boundaries of what's possible

Celebrate — these generate vital learning

How do leaders make this real, not just words on a wall?

Celebrating Intelligent Failure

Leadership Actions

  • Share post-mortems as learning, not blame
  • Reward engineers who ran experiments
  • Ask "What did we learn?" not "Who did this?"
  • Share your own failures publicly

What This Unlocks

  • Engineers take calculated risks
  • Problems surface early and small
  • Innovation becomes organic
  • Agency levels rise across the team

Safety gives people permission to act. Competence gives them the ability.

How to Increase Competence

Deliberate Practice

Tight Feedback Loops

Deploy to staging daily. Run chaos experiments. Measure everything.

Cross-Pollination

Learn adjacent domains. A platform engineer who understands product is 10x more effective.

Build & Teach

Build internal tools. Write runbooks. Mentor juniors. Teaching forces deep understanding.

It starts by changing how we think. Most teams think in tickets. High-agency teams think in systems.

Systems Thinking

Linear Thinking

  • "More servers = better performance"
  • Ignores cascading effects
  • Fixes symptoms, not root causes

Systems Thinking

  • "More servers might increase coordination overhead"
  • Maps feedback loops and dependencies
  • Considers second and third-order effects

Linear thinking fixes symptoms. Systems thinking fixes root causes.

When you apply systems thinking to your platform, the first thing you realize: it's not a project. It's a product.

Platform as a Product

Project Thinking

  • Ship features from a backlog
  • "Done" when the ticket closes
  • Success = delivery on time
  • Users are an afterthought
→

Product Thinking

  • Understand developer needs deeply
  • "Done" when user outcome is achieved
  • Success = developer velocity
  • Users inform every decision

Your users are internal developers. Treat them like paying customers.

So if developers are our customers, how well are we serving them today?

The Developer Experience Gap

What developers want

  • Fast feedback loops
  • Self-service capabilities
  • Clear, current documentation
  • Guardrails, not gates

What we often give them

  • Complex tools requiring training
  • Approval workflows for everything
  • Tribal knowledge via Slack DMs
  • Checkpoints that slow them down

Closing this gap is where high-agency platform teams create the most impact.

But even teams with good intentions fall into two predictable traps...

Overcoming Product Biases

Two biases that plague smart teams

Execution Orientation Fallacy

Building what is easiest today rather than tackling complex, high-value architectural problems.

"We shipped 12 features!" — but the DB migration that would 10x perf is untouched.

Bias-for-Building Fallacy

Rushing to code without understanding if developers actually need the tool.

"We built an amazing CLI!" — but nobody uses it because devs wanted a web UI.

The antidote to both biases: ruthless prioritization of where you spend energy.

The 10-30-50 Rule

Building T-shaped engineers for maximum leverage

10%

Core Mastery

Top 10% in one core skill. Your superpower.

e.g., Kubernetes, distributed systems

30%

Complementary

Top 30% in a skill that amplifies your core.

e.g., Security, product management

50%

Breadth

Top 50% in a third for cross-functional fluency.

e.g., Data engineering, ML/AI

These are percentile ranks (how you compare to peers), not time allocation. They don't add up to 100%.
